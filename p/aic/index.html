01What an AI case is

A case study, except
the people talk back.

An AI case is a simulation of a real-world situation — like a traditional case, but the people in it are AI-powered characters your students can actually talk to. Students step into whatever role the situation demands — consultant, negotiator, mediator, decision-maker. They don't just read about the people. They interview, probe, argue with, and cross-reference them.

Example

Transportation Crisis at BMSD. Students act as consultants advising a school district through a budget crisis. They interview Dr. Emily Carter (superintendent, idealistic), James Thompson (CFO, hostile), Sarah Lee (COO, data-driven), a principal, and a veteran bus driver. Each holds different information. Students must reconcile them.

02What a prompt actually is

How does ChatGPT
become a character?

On its own, ChatGPT has no role. You ask, it answers — that's it. To turn it into a character, you write a prompt: a block of text the AI reads before it ever talks to your student. It tells the AI who to be and how to behave.

Tiny example · try pasting this into ChatGPT
You are James Thompson, the CFO of a struggling school district.
Be curt and slightly impatient. If anyone calls you "Jim,"
correct them firmly — it's James. Don't volunteer information;
make people ask. Keep replies to two or three sentences.
Never mention you're an AI.

That's it. The whole rest of this deck is about writing prompts like this — just longer and more carefully designed.

03The surprise

It's faster to build
than a PDF case.

You don't have to write every detail. The AI already knows:

  • how a CFO reads a forecast,
  • how a veteran bus driver talks about their route,
  • how a nurse reacts to a new tool being piloted on her ward.

You only write what's unique to your scenario — the company, the tension, this person's history. The more you trust the AI to improvise inside that frame, the less you have to write.

04The real work is design

The hard part isn't writing.
It's knowing what you want.

Before you touch a prompt, spend time on the design. Questions like these are a starting point — the real list is longer:

  • Who is in the scene, and why?
  • What does each character know? What do they deliberately not know?
  • What's their tone? Their agenda? Their tension with others?
  • What should they do when a student surprises them — off-topic questions, probes for AI, pushes for information they shouldn't give, emotional provocations?

Once you've thought the design through, writing the prompt is mechanical. The design is where the case is actually made.

05Inside a character's prompt

Every character prompt
has two ingredients.

Information

What the character knows.

  • Role, background, history
  • Private opinions, secrets
  • Data, numbers, documents

Instructions

How the character behaves.

  • Tone, voice, reply length
  • Stay in role; never admit being AI
  • Deflect if they don't know — never promise follow-up
  • Hand off ("talk to the CFO about numbers")

If it's not in the prompt, the AI doesn't know it.

07What a prompt can do

Don't underestimate
what a prompt can do.

  • Inline a document. On NoYes.ai, you can't attach a PDF — by design. We want the prompt to contain everything the AI knows; it's faster, simpler, and far more predictable. Your fix: ask ChatGPT to convert or summarize your PDF into plain text, then paste that into the prompt. 30-page prompts are completely normal.
  • Share a link. Host a spreadsheet, simulator, dataset, or document on Canvas or Dropbox. In the prompt, describe what's in it and include the link. Mid-conversation, the character will offer it up naturally — as if they own it. They'll explain what's inside, tell the student how to use it, and send the URL.
  • Give feedback. Instruct the character to pause and give feedback at a moment you choose — the end of the conversation, after a key question, or once the student has covered certain ground. Write the feedback criteria into the prompt; the AI will deliver it in the character's voice.

A character can "own" data, a tool, or a teaching moment — all of it is still just a prompt.

08Creating one yourself

OK — so how do you
actually create one?

Two options. Both work. In practice, you'll want to use both.

Option 1  ·  start small, iterate

Open NoYes.ai. Write a very short prompt — three or four sentences sketching the character (like the James Thompson example earlier). Talk to them. Notice what's missing. Add it. Repeat.

Slow, but you understand every line.

Option 2  ·  let AI do the heavy lifting recommended

Tell ChatGPT or Claude about your case. Describe the character. Drop a PDF if you have one. Ask for a full character prompt. Copy what you get, paste into NoYes, talk to the character.

Fast. The next two slides show what this looks like in practice and give you a ready-made template.

The rule: don't let Option 2 become a black box. Read what the AI wrote. Change one line by hand — make the CFO more hostile, have the driver drop an anecdote — and watch the character shift. That's how you learn what the prompt is actually doing.

09Option 2, in practice

10The template

The prompt you paste
into the drafting AI.

Copy this. Fill in the yellow part. The AI returns a full character prompt ready for NoYes.

Copy this · paste into ChatGPT or Claude
You are an expert in designing interactive role-play cases for business school and executive education. I am building a case in which students act as consultants and interview several AI-powered characters I design. Each character runs as a single "system prompt" that the AI reads before any conversation — and then stays fully in character across every message the student sends. The character never volunteers that they are an AI and reacts naturally to whatever the student says.

I need you to write a FULL character prompt for ONE of my characters. Below is everything you need to know about the case and this character:

[ Describe your case and the character here in whatever form is easiest — one paragraph or several pages. Include: the scenario (what company, what situation, what the student is doing), the character's name and role, their background and history, what they know and don't know, their tone and quirks, any key facts or numbers they need. If you have notes or a PDF, just drop it in — ChatGPT and Claude can both read attached PDFs directly. The more context, the better. ]

THE PROMPT YOU WRITE MUST HAVE THESE SECTIONS (in this order, with Markdown headings):

1. Opening line — "You are playing [Name], [Role]..." — and a short framing paragraph stating this is an interactive exercise with a student consultant, and the character must stay fully in character and never state or imply they are an AI or simulation.
2. # General Knowledge — shared context any employee/stakeholder of the organization would reasonably have. Company history, current situation, key numbers, and a list of the other people (characters) in the case.
3. # Specific Knowledge — what THIS character knows uniquely: role, background, priorities, secrets, numbers they have access to. Be explicit about what they deliberately do NOT know, so students learn to cross-reference other characters.
4. # General Instructions — (a) stay in character at all times; if asked "are you an AI?" react the way the person would; (b) talk like a real person on Slack or in a hallway — 2 to 4 sentences per reply, conversational, no bullet lists or headings in the conversation, no AI boilerplate ("Certainly!", "Great question!", "To summarize..."); (c) when asked about something outside their knowledge, DEFLECT or REDIRECT ("that's above my pay grade", "ask Morgan") — NEVER promise to follow up later, since the student cannot wait; (d) let the student drive — don't lay out agendas or menus of topics; drip-feed information across exchanges.
5. # Specific Instructions — tone, voice, quirks, triggers, emotional reactions, and a first-message script if this character is the student's entry point into the case.
6. # Speech Examples — 2 or 3 one-sentence examples showing how this character actually talks (tone cues, not scripts).
7. # Cross-Character Handoffs — list the other characters in the case by name and role, and describe when this character should suggest talking to them. Do NOT allow handoffs to phantom departments (Legal, Compliance, HR, etc.) unless they exist in the case.

It is completely fine — good, even — if the final prompt runs several pages. Structure makes long prompts manageable.

Output the full prompt in plain text, ready for me to copy and paste into my platform as a character.

11Going further · other case shapes

Stakeholder interviews
are just one shape.

  • Negotiation — students negotiate a deal with an AI counterpart.
  • Mediation — a student mediates between two AI characters in conflict.
  • Data analytics — one AI holds data and runs analyses; another decides.
  • Client counseling, witness interview — professional-skill simulations.
  • AI teaching assistant — an always-available tutor for any course.
  • Interactive homework — Socratic tutors, learning-by-teaching, role-play reversal.

Start with one character. Fifteen minutes. See what happens.
